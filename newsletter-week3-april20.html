01 · Glossary

Algebra Vocabulary at a Glance

Before the deep-dive definitions below, here is a one-screen reference students can bookmark. If a word shows up in a problem and feels slippery, the table is the fastest place to anchor it.

TermQuick Definition
VariableA letter (like x) that represents an unknown or changing number.
ConstantA fixed value that does not change (e.g., 7, −3, π).
CoefficientThe numerical factor multiplying a variable (the 4 in 4x).
TermA single number, variable, or product of them — pieces separated by + or −.
ExpressionA combination of numbers, variables, and operations with no equals sign.
EquationA statement that two expressions are equal (has an = sign).
InequalityA statement comparing two expressions with <, >, ≤, or ≥.
PolynomialA sum of terms of the form aₙxⁿ with whole-number exponents.
Monomial / Binomial / TrinomialPolynomials with 1, 2, or 3 terms respectively.
FactorSomething being multiplied; also, to rewrite a sum as a product.
Like TermsTerms with the same variables raised to the same powers (3x² and −5x²).
SolutionA value that makes an equation or inequality true.
FunctionA rule that assigns exactly one output to each input.
SlopeThe steepness of a line: rise over run, or (y₂ − y₁)/(x₂ − x₁).
InterceptWhere a graph crosses an axis (x-intercept or y-intercept).
02 · Vocabulary · Deep Dive

Key Algebra Definitions, Explained

Students often "know" these words without being able to use them. Each card below pairs a precise definition with a concrete example — these are the ones I drill most often in office hours.

Variable Core

A symbol — most often a letter — that stands in for a number we don't yet know or that can take on many values. A variable isn't "unknown forever"; it's a placeholder we're going to pin down or let vary deliberately.

3x + 5 = 20 → here, x is the variable
Example: In the formula for the area of a rectangle, A = ℓw, both ℓ and w are variables — they change from rectangle to rectangle.

Coefficient Core

The number that multiplies a variable in a term. If no number is written, the coefficient is understood to be 1. A negative sign in front of a term belongs to the coefficient.

−7x² + x − 4 → coefficients: −7, 1, and −4 (constant term)
Why it matters: When combining like terms, you add the coefficients — not the variables: 3x + 5x = 8x, not 8x².

Expression vs. Equation Don't mix these up

An expression is a mathematical phrase — there is nothing to solve, only to simplify or evaluate. An equation sets two expressions equal to each other — now there is something to solve.

Expression: 2x + 3 Equation: 2x + 3 = 11
Rule of thumb: If there is an equals sign, it's an equation (or an identity). If not, it's an expression. You can simplify an expression; you solve an equation.

Polynomial Structure

A sum of terms where each term is a constant times a variable raised to a whole-number (non-negative integer) power. The highest exponent that appears is the degree of the polynomial.

4x³ − 2x² + x − 7 → degree 3 (cubic), 4 terms
Not a polynomial: 3/x, √x, or x^(−1) — negative or fractional exponents on a variable are not allowed.

Like Terms Simplifying

Terms with exactly the same variable parts (same variables, same powers). Only like terms can be added or subtracted.

5x²y and −3x²y → like terms 5x²y and −3xy² → not like
Example: 5x²y + 4x − 3x²y = (5 − 3)x²y + 4x = 2x²y + 4x.

Factor / Factoring Two meanings

As a noun, a factor is something being multiplied. As a verb, to factor is to rewrite a sum as a product — the reverse of distributing.

x² − 9 = (x − 3)(x + 3) 6x² + 9x = 3x(2x + 3)
Why it matters: Factoring turns "hard to solve" into "easy to solve" — a product is zero only when one of its factors is zero.

Function Cornerstone

A rule that takes each input and returns exactly one output. No input is allowed two different outputs. We write f(x) for "the output of the function f when the input is x."

f(x) = 2x + 1 → f(3) = 2(3) + 1 = 7
Vertical line test: If any vertical line crosses a graph more than once, the graph is not a function.

Slope & Intercept Lines

The slope measures how steep a line is — how much y changes per unit change in x. The y-intercept is the point where the line crosses the y-axis (where x = 0).

Slope: m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁) Line: y = mx + b
Example: The line y = 2x − 3 has slope 2 and y-intercept (0, −3). Positive slope ⇒ going up, negative ⇒ going down, zero ⇒ flat, undefined ⇒ vertical.
03 · Transition

Bridging Middle School Math to High School

🎒 For 7th & 8th graders 👩‍🏫 For their teachers

The single biggest shift between middle school math and high school algebra is not "harder numbers" — it is a change in what counts as an answer. In middle school, arithmetic rewards finding the number. In high school, algebra rewards explaining the structure: why the same rule applies to every number.

The students who transition smoothly are almost never the fastest computers. They are the ones with solid fluency in the skills below and the habit of asking "why does this step work?" after every line.

The honest test: Can the student explain what each letter means in a formula and why a step is legal — not just whether it produced the right answer? That is the readiness signal.
04 · Prerequisites

Skills to Solidify Before High School Algebra

Send this list home over the summer. A student who owns these comfortably is set up for a successful first year of high school math.

🔢 Number Sense

  • Fluent operations with integers (including negatives)
  • Fractions: add, subtract, multiply, divide — no calculator
  • Decimals & percents: convert between forms
  • Order of operations (PEMDAS) with nested grouping
  • Prime factorization, GCF, LCM
  • Absolute value as distance from zero

🧩 Pre-Algebra Mechanics

  • Distributive property: a(b + c) = ab + ac
  • Combining like terms
  • Solving one- and two-step equations
  • Basic laws of exponents (product, quotient, power rules)
  • Evaluating expressions by substitution
  • Translating words into expressions and equations

📐 Geometry & Measurement

  • Perimeter, area, and volume of basic shapes
  • The Pythagorean theorem: a² + b² = c²
  • Angle relationships: complementary, supplementary, vertical
  • Similar figures and scale factor
  • Units and unit conversion

📊 Graphs & Data

  • Coordinate plane: plotting (x, y) points confidently
  • Reading and creating tables of values
  • Graphing a linear equation by slope-intercept
  • Identifying slope from two points or a graph
  • Mean, median, mode, range
  • Proportional vs. non-proportional relationships
05 · For Teachers

Three Habits That Smooth the Transition

A short note for middle school teachers preparing students for the jump. None of these require rewriting the curriculum — only small shifts in how skills are reinforced.

① Insist on vocabulary precision

Don't let "cancel" or "move it over" substitute for the real operation. When a student says "cancel the 3," press them to say "divide both sides by 3." Algebra teachers will thank you — and so will their students.

② Make justifications routine

After every step, ask: what property did you use? Distributive? Additive inverse? This is the single most useful habit students can carry into Algebra I, and it costs nothing but 30 seconds per problem.

③ Treat variables as numbers, not letters

Students who think of x as "a letter" will freeze. Students who think of x as "a number we just don't know yet" will reason. Model the substitution habit every week.

④ Slow down on negatives and fractions

Ninety percent of "Algebra mistakes" in the first semester trace back to a sign error or a fraction error — not new content. Quick mixed-practice warmups pay dividends later.

Talk to a high school colleague. A 30-minute conversation with your feeder high school's Algebra I team will tell you exactly which prerequisites their first unit assumes. Align on those, and the handoff gets dramatically easier.
06 · Science News

Headlines Worth a Classroom Minute

A few short items from this week's science news — all worth a five-minute "what's happening in the world" spot at the start of class.

🪐 Euclid Telescope Releases First Deep-Sky Catalog

ESA's Euclid mission released its first broad catalog of galaxy shapes and positions this week — a data set designed to help map the distribution of dark matter and dark energy across billions of light-years.

🧬 CRISPR Therapy Clears a Rare Liver Disorder

A clinical trial using a next-generation CRISPR base-editing therapy reported durable remission in patients with a hereditary liver disease, extending the list of conditions where gene editing has moved from "promising" to "treating."

🌊 Record Ocean Heat Persists Into April

Sea-surface temperatures across several ocean basins remain at record levels for this time of year, prompting forecasters to revise their Atlantic hurricane season outlook upward.

🔋 Sodium-Ion Batteries Hit the Grid

The first utility-scale sodium-ion battery installation in North America came online this week. Less energy-dense than lithium, but cheaper, safer, and built from abundant materials — a promising pairing for grid storage.
