📍 Intro to Scatterplots
A scatterplot displays the relationship between 2 quantitative variables measured on the same individual. Each individual becomes an ordered pair (x, y).
Explanatory Variable (x)
= INDEPENDENT variable
EXPLAINS or causes changes in the response. Goes on the x-axis.
Response Variable (y)
= DEPENDENT variable
Measures the outcome or result. Goes on the y-axis.

📊 Correlation
Correlation measures how well a line represents a set of data points. Denoted by r (correlation coefficient), always between −1 and 1. Described by direction and strength.

📈 Line of Best Fit
A line that lies as close as possible to all data points. Calculated via linear regression. About half of non-line points should be above, half below.
Form: y = ax + b  |  a = slope  |  b = y-intercept
Slope (a) means…
As x increases by 1, y increases (or decreases) by a
Y-Intercept (b) means…
When x = 0, the predicted y-value is b (check context!)

🔍 Correlation, Causation & Lurking Variables
Correlation does NOT mean causation. A lurking variable can hide in the background and explain an observed relationship.
Key Distinction: Just because two variables are correlated does not mean one causes the other to change. Example: video games ↔ violence — this is correlation, not causation. Smoking → lung cancer — this IS causation.

📐 Residuals & R²
Residuals measure how far each actual point is from the predicted value. R² tells us what percentage of the variation can be explained by our regression line.
Residual Formula
residual = actual y − predicted y
✅ Positive residual → actual is above the line (heavier backpack than predicted)
❌ Negative residual → actual is below the line (lighter backpack than predicted)
⭐ Residuals should be small and close to zero for a good fit
R-Squared (R²) Formula
R² = r²
R² = the percentage of the difference in y-values that can be explained by the regression line.
100 − R² = percentage due to lurking variables
